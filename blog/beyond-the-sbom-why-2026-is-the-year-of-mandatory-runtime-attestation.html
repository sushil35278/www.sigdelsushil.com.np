Beyond the SBOM: Why 2026 is the Year of Mandatory Runtime Attestation

By Sushil Sigdel | 18 July 2026

The Illusion of the Static Manifest

For the last three years, the industry obsessed over the Software Bill of Materials (SBOM). We generated JSON files, archived them in S3 buckets, and told our auditors we were secure. But as we move through 2026, a uncomfortable consensus has emerged among senior architects: a static manifest is nothing more than a receipt for a meal that might have already gone bad. In my time consulting for fintech firms in Tokyo and working on disaster recovery systems in Kathmandu, I’ve seen that the gap between what is 'built' and what is 'running' is where 90% of modern breaches live.

The debate in 2026 has shifted from inventory to attestation. It is no longer enough to know which version of log4j or its successor you are using; you must cryptographically prove that the binary running in your cluster is the exact same one that passed through your hardened build pipeline, with no unauthorized mutations in between.

The Rise of Ephemeral OIDC and Keyless Signing

One of the most significant shifts we are seeing in 2026 is the total abandonment of long-lived signing keys. In the past, we treated GPG keys like family heirlooms—they were leaked, forgotten, or stored in insecure environment variables. Inspired by the Sigstore project's evolution, engineering leaders are now mandating keyless signing via OpenID Connect (OIDC).

When a developer in an Osaka-based engineering hub pushes code, the CI/CD runner (like GitHub Actions or GitLab Runner) requests a short-lived identity token. This token is used to sign the artifact. The 'public key' is actually a temporary entry in a transparency log. This eliminates the 'stolen key' vector entirely. If the build didn't happen in the authorized environment, the signature cannot exist.

Implementing Runtime Policy Enforcement

The real friction point in 2026 isn't the signing—it's the enforcement. We are moving away from 'detect and alert' toward 'deny by default' at the kernel level. Using tools like Kyverno or Open Policy Agent (OPA) combined with eBPF, organizations are now blocking containers that lack a valid, verifiable attestation from the build system.

Consider this OPA policy snippet that enforces a 'no attestation, no execution' rule for a Kubernetes admission controller:


package kubernetes.admission

import data.lib.sigstore

deny[msg] {
    input.request.kind.kind == "Pod"
    container := input.request.object.spec.containers[_]
    image := container.image
    
    # Verify the image has a valid attestation from our trusted builder
    not sigstore.verify_attestation(image, {
        "issuer": "https://token.actions.githubusercontent.com",
        "subject_reg_exp": "https://github.com/my-org/.*",
        "predicate_type": "https://slsa.dev/provenance/v0.2"
    })
    
    msg := sprintf("Deployment denied: Image %v lacks valid cryptographic provenance.", [image])
}

This isn't just theory. In late 2025, a major regional bank in South Asia avoided a significant supply chain compromise because their runtime environment rejected a malicious image that had been pushed to their private registry via a compromised developer account. The image was 'valid' in terms of registry permissions, but it lacked the CI-signed attestation required by the cluster.

The 'Nepal Protocol': Security in Low-Trust Environments

Working on infrastructure in Kathmandu taught me a vital lesson: assume the network is hostile and the power will go out. In cybersecurity, this translates to 'Offline-First Verification.' In 2026, we are seeing the adoption of local-only verification nodes. Instead of calling out to a centralized authority to check a signature, clusters maintain a local cache of the transparency log. This reduces latency and prevents a 'denial of service' on your security stack from taking down your entire production environment.

Pro Tips for Engineering Leaders

  • Mandate SLSA Level 3: Don't just settle for signed images. Aim for the Supply-chain Levels for Software Artifacts (SLSA) Level 3, which requires a non-falsifiable build record.
  • Audit your 'Build-Time' Dependencies: 70% of vulnerabilities in 2026 come from build-time tools (compilers, linters, and scripts) rather than the libraries linked into the final binary. Use hermetic builds where the build environment has no network access.
  • Shift to Content-Addressable Storage: Stop referring to images by tags like :latest or even :v1.2.0. Use the SHA-256 digest in your manifests. It is the only way to ensure the code you tested is the code you deployed.

Future Predictions: The Compiler as Auditor

By 2028, I expect to see 'Attestation-Aware Compilers' become the standard. Imagine a Rust or Go compiler that refuses to link a library unless that library provides a valid, verifiable chain of custody back to its source. We are already seeing experimental forks of the LLVM toolchain that embed cryptographic hashes of the source code directly into the ELF headers of the compiled binary.

The debate over SBOMs is over; the era of active, runtime provenance is here. If your 2026 roadmap doesn't include moving toward a Zero-Trust build pipeline, you aren't just behind the curve—you are leaving the door unlocked for the most sophisticated threat actors we've ever faced.

Conclusion

The shift from static security to dynamic attestation is the defining architectural challenge of the mid-2020s. As leaders, we must move past the 'compliance checkbox' mentality and embrace the reality that our software is only as secure as the proof we can provide for its origin. Are you ready to enforce the 'deny-by-default' rule in your production clusters?
